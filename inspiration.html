We learn from mistakes.


  • Best Buy: The small change of allowing people to skip sign-up during checkout resulted in $300 million extra revenue per year.
    – The $300 million button
  • Netflix: Netflix tried to cut a function that was confusing and useless, but users strongly objected. They kept the feature.
    – Features are a one-way street
  • Target: Target.com bought Amazon’s customer review software. In the first month after Harry Potter and the Deathly Hallows came out, Amazon got 1805 reviews, whereas Target received only 3 (both retailers sold about 2 million copies).
    – Revealing design treasures from Amazon
  • Kleenex: Originally marketed as a disposable face towel to remove make-up, the manufacturer found out 2 years later that the majority of people were using Kleenex to blow their noses. They started advertising it as a handkerchief, which doubled their sales figures.
    – History of Kleenex tissue
  • Google: Buzz was tested with 20,000 Google employees and still launched with a feature that generated loads of complaints until the feature was dropped. It ran smoothly with tons of employees, but they weren’t the real users.
    – You are not your user
  • Walmart: Walmart decluttered their aisles, which improved customer satisfaction but reduced inventory by 15%. It cost Walmart $1.85 billion to listen to what their customers said, instead of what they actually did.
    – Walmart loses $1.85 billion in sales per customers’ request
  • BMW: The iDrive in-car infotainment control system started with a minimal approach: the only control was a turn knob. Later on, due to extensive consumer feedback, 2 extra buttons were added. Now, it features 8 dedicated buttons, greatly improving the user experience and driving safety.
    – iDrive
